The third piece of a company's memory system focuses on preserving organizational context before it gets compressed into artifacts. While factual memory (artifacts, tickets, CRM entries) tracks what happened, interaction memory preserves why things happened and the constraints that mattered.

Most critical company decisions don't happen in databases—they happen in conversations. A customer complaint on a call, a sales promise made in Slack, a manager's mention of blockers, a founder changing priorities. By the time these generate tickets or CRM updates, the real context (the chain of thought) has evaporated.

Interaction memory captures the trace of how a group moved from partial information to judgment: why a decision was made, how people expected work to be done, which assumptions were fragile, and what was left unsaid.

## Key Takeaways
- Factual memory (artifacts) and interaction memory (conversations) serve different purposes
- Organizations lose context when decision conversations aren't preserved separately from outcomes
- Interaction memory is the "chain of thought" that explains organizational judgment
- Most company decisions happen in meetings and messages, not in documented procedures
- Without preserving interaction context, teams repeatedly lose the "why" behind decisions
