Ashwin Gopinath built "Company Brain" (originally pitched as "Enterprise General Intelligence") over the past year with a simple founder problem at its core: how to understand what's happening inside the company without relying on status updates, secondhand summaries, and scattered context across meetings, messages, documents, and people. The initial naive approach—having an agent call around and ask everyone what's happening—broke quickly because people don't respond well to bot interrogation.

The actual lesson is deeper: Company Brain became less about surveillance and more about ambient awareness. The real insight is that if intelligence becomes commoditized (every company gets access to similar AI), then competitive advantage comes from how the company works—its processes, culture, and ability to act on information. Building organizational intelligence requires fundamentally rethinking how information flows, who has access to what, and how decisions get made. The article traces the journey from naive centralized intelligence to discovering that real organizational brains require distributed decision-making and trust-based information architecture.

## Key Takeaways
- Founders struggle with information fragmentation across meetings, messages, documents, and people
- Centralized agent-based surveillance doesn't work—people resist being interrogated by bots
- If intelligence commoditizes, competitive advantage shifts to organizational process and culture
- Real Company Brain is about ambient awareness and accessible context, not surveillance
- Information architecture and trust structures matter more than the intelligence system itself
