Companies forget not just what happened or why decisions were made, but how work actually gets done—especially work that crosses people, systems, approvals, and time. The most important capability of a Company Brain at the action layer is knowing when NOT to act.

Action memory is not simply workflow memory. It's the memory of when a workflow should wake up, when it should not, and what should happen at each step. A useful automation system acts because the context says it should, and stays still when it should not. Doing nothing is a first-class action.

For example: a customer needs a pricing exception. Everyone may know the account, conversation, and business reason. But that doesn't tell the company who approves the exception, whether legal needs to review, which system gets updated, whether finance needs a note, who tells the customer, or what happens if the discount crosses 15% (where finance approval becomes mandatory).

Without action memory, automation becomes unreliable and potentially harmful. A system that cannot choose to do nothing cannot be trusted to do anything intentionally.

## Key Takeaways
- Action memory is about knowing when NOT to act, not just how to act
- Organizational context determines if automation should execute
- A 15% discount threshold might trigger different approval paths
- Trust in automation requires understanding of organizational constraints
- Doing nothing is a first-class action in company workflows
